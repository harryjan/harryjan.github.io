Regulating consumer first value chains

Originally published on Medium on November 6, 2017.

Policy makers have a fine line to walk

Traditional regulation & competition law was designed to prevent collusion and exploitation of the consumer. To protect against a single entity which controlled more than X% of the market using its position to:

A. Exploit consumers lack of choice to drive higher profits

B. Build an anti-competitive shield to protect those profits

Today’s Technology giants (exemplified by the GAMA grouping) have developed models which turn this on its head. They have build their empires by:

A. ‘Exploiting’ commercial partners lack of direct consumer access to drive down costs (Commoditising Producers) and profit from scale

B. Building shields around accessing consumers to protect profits derived from that scale

C. Vertically integrating using derived profits on the most profitable components of the value chain

The important thing to note here is that the model is on the surface, beneficial to consumers.

A. They get more choice (Lower barriers of entry for producers)

B. They pay less (Competition between commoditised producers and efficencies of scale from integrated supply chains)

C. They have a better customer experience (Due to the investment enabled by the efficencies of scale)

Future Policy

So what should policy makers be concerned about if the eCommerce players are providing such improvements in consumer value?

I believe the primary task should be to protect innovators/competition to the technology monoliths from being swamped by these organisations, how; I don’t know, but a few questions we might ask are.

Innovators —How can we protect innovative tech-savy producers creating new vertically integrating offerings that capture direct access to consumers (i.e Harry’s Razors) from being subsumed into Technology oligarchies?

Data — How can we protect tangential organisations whose data and access to consumers will disproportionately benefit the Technology leaders?

Vertical Integration- How can we protect organisations (or even sectors) who are components in Technology leaders value chains from being subsumed (i.e Delivery companies).

The real challenge for policy makers is that the decisions they have to make now are far more nuanced and have far wider timescales for consideration that in the past. With the rate of technological change now occurring, what may be seen as an unimportant tangential M&A purchase today, could be a critical piece of value capture at a 5 year timescale (I.e Facebook’s purchase of WhatsApp).

The line between protecting consumers today and protecting them in the future has never been so fine.
